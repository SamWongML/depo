v3 · Connected & Self-Correcting Edition · June 2026

An engine that sees the hidden connections — and learns from you each night.

Three upgrades to the same disciplined nightly system. (1) A connection graph so when a holding's biggest position falls, the engine instantly shows every other fund on your lists exposed to the same name, supplier, or driver — the overlap you can't see by eye. (2) A feedback loop: each morning you agree, disagree, or correct the read, and tonight's digest is conditioned on what you taught it — continual adaptation, no fine-tuning. (3) A modern, point-in-time data spine — bitemporal time-series, a local lakehouse, vectors, and a temporal graph, all embedded. The philosophy is unchanged and deliberate: descriptive factor states, never a fitted composite — the LLM interprets, it does not optimize, so there is almost nothing to overfit.

◆ The design principle that makes it durable

A durable system optimizes for a trustworthy nightly verdict, not a clever prediction. v3 keeps the three rules that don't decay — numbers as legible states, two-sided dated evidence, point-in-time everything — and adds two that compound: connections you can audit (most are exact graph look-ups, not model guesses) and memory you govern (the engine adapts to your corrections, but validity windows and human-reviewed promotion stop bad feedback from rotting it). Themes rotate; the process stays the same.

The honest frame carries over: signals shorten your lag, not your risk. The engine surfaces a sourced, calibrated, conditional lean — and now a map of what moves with what — each morning. You remain the only thing that clicks buy.

03 / THE FACTOR STATES

Simple, strong, descriptive — never a fitted scoreThe numeric core, kept deliberately un-optimized. Overfitting is a disease of optimization; if you never fit weights to past P&L, there is almost nothing left to overfit.

v3 keeps signal_core's central choice: each raw input becomes a descriptive state — its current reading ranked against its own history and bucketed into a band (extreme_low … extreme_high), carrying a direction, a dated evidence pointer, and a valid flag. There is no weighted composite anywhere. The LLM reads the states and judges, in context and against the news, when each one is economically relevant — it does not compute or optimize them. Three construction rules survive; the factor list is sharpened by the 2026 evidence.

  • Rank against own history, in logic-based bands. Cut-points are constants chosen for economic meaning, never tuned to returns. A failed/stale factor becomes valid:false and the model is told to ignore it — missing ≠ bad, and one broken factor never sinks the snapshot.
  • Read direction, never assume "high = good". In A-shares a stretched up-move is reversal-down risk, not bullish continuation. The state encodes which way "high" points.
  • A handful is enough. The factor-zoo collapses to ~13 themes; the practitioner core is market, value, quality, momentum, low-vol. Naive 1/N beats 14 optimization models out-of-sample (DeMiguel). Add a factor only when it would have changed a past decision you got wrong — not because it is theoretically blessed.

★ Why this beats the old dual-momentum composite (the 2026 evidence)

Crowding predicts crashes, not means. Out-of-sample (2001–2024), crowded reversal factors show ~1.7–1.8× higher crash probability, and crowding-based selection earns no alpha — so crowding belongs as a risk gauge, exactly where it sits here. Momentum decayed from ~10%/yr in the 1990s to ~2% and is now crowded with the widest winner-minus-loser dispersion since 1990 — so it stays a gate, never the engine. And short-term reversal — driven by turnover and Amihud illiquidity — is the robust exception that still diversifies, which is why it is the A-share workhorse. The states-not-composite design turns these findings into structure instead of a knob to overfit.

The trend gate is the one hard rule: if a theme is below its 200-day MA, the LLM caps the lean at Hold/Avoid no matter how attractive the story. Never buy a downtrend. Everything else is judgment over states — humbled by a stated base rate, and now by your own past corrections (§06).

05 / THE CONNECTION GRAPH & GRAPHRAG

Surfacing the overlap you can't seeThe upgrade you asked for: fund A holds B; B is falling; which other funds on my lists also hold B — and what else moves with it.

This is two capabilities, and keeping them separate is what makes it trustworthy. Most of the value is a deterministic look-up — provably correct, no model involved. A thin layer of GraphRAG sits on top only for the fuzzy, second-order links a query can't name.

One falling name (中际旭创) sits inside three funds on your lists at once — invisible by eye, one hop in the graph. A second hop reaches the shared driver, where a QDII you hold is also exposed.

Capability 1 — deterministic look-through exact · no LLM

The graph stores (:Fund)-[:HOLDS {weight, as_of}]->(:Security)-[:EXPOSED_TO]->(:Driver|:Theme) from the holdings feeds in §04. "B is falling — who else holds B, and what's my look-through?" is then a one-hop query that is fast, exact, and auditable:

// every other item on my lists exposed to a name that fell today, with look-through weight MATCH (s:Security)<-[h:HOLDS]-(f:Fund) WHERE s.ret_1d <= -0.05 AND f.on_my_list = true RETURN s.name, s.ret_1d, collect({fund:f.name, weight:h.weight}) AS exposed_funds, sum(h.weight * f.my_position) AS total_lookthrough ORDER BY total_lookthrough DESC;

This is the engine's most reliable feature precisely because no model is in the path — it is set arithmetic over disclosed holdings. It catches the "illusion of diversification" (three funds, one bet) directly, and feeds the connections[] block in the dashboard.

Capability 2 — GraphRAG for the hidden, second-order links fuzzy · LLM over the graph

Some contagion isn't a shared holding — it's a shared supplier, shared end-demand, a competitor, or the same macro driver two hops away. Here the LLM reasons over the falling node's graph neighbourhood plus the news. On complex, multi-hop financial questions, graph-grounded retrieval reaches ~86% accuracy versus ~59–72% for vector-only, and ~3.4× the accuracy of plain RAG — because it follows relationships instead of matching loose text. For a single-user nightly system the right implementation is LightRAG (dual-mode graph+vector, cheap incremental updates as the graph grows nightly, ~70–90% of heavyweight GraphRAG quality at a fraction of the cost), with LanceDB as the embedded vector store underneath.

The schema (FinDKG-inspired, kept small)

// entity types Fund/ETF · Security/Company · Theme · Driver · Supplier · Region · Person · Event · MacroFactor // relations HOLDS · EXPOSED_TO · SUPPLIES · COMPETES_WITH · DRIVES · IMPACTS · CORRELATES_WITH · LISTED_IN · LEADS // every edge carries {as_of, source, valid_from, valid_to} → the graph is TEMPORAL, point-in-time like everything else

Build the text-derived edges (SUPPLIES, DRIVES, IMPACTS, LEADS) with the LLM-as-extractor pattern (FinDKG's ICKG showed this runs even on a 7B model; you can use Claude). Build the hard edges (HOLDS, LISTED_IN) straight from the §04 holdings feeds — no extraction, no error. Keep rolling weekly snapshots and use graph centrality to watch which node is becoming systemically important to your book — the same technique FinDKG used to track Covid contagion week by week.

★ The graduate tier (optional, human-reviewed — like Qlib was for factors)

Once the temporal graph has history, a graph neural net (FinDKG's KGTransformer) can do temporal link prediction: query (?, produce, AI) to rank companies genuinely creating a theme, or (AI, impact, ?) to rank who's exposed — a forward-looking thematic basket, ~15% better on link-prediction metrics than prior temporal-KG models. Treat any GNN-surfaced edge exactly like an RD-Agent factor: a candidate for review, never a drop-in. The exact look-through (Capability 1) remains the trustworthy default; the GNN is upside, not load-bearing.

Tech honesty (see §11): the obvious embedded graph pick, KùzuDB, was archived in October 2025. For an embedded, single-writer store use its maintained successor LadybugDB; if you'd rather run a local server, Neo4j Community has the most mature GraphRAG ecosystem; and Zep/Graphiti is a temporal-KG engine that can serve the graph and the feedback memory (§06) from one technology.

06 / THE FEEDBACK LOOP

It learns from you each night — without fine-tuningYou asked for a digest that takes your feedback and adjusts its judgment the next day. The modern way to do that is memory, not training.

Fine-tuning a model on a handful of your daily reactions is the wrong tool — slow, expensive, and prone to forgetting. The 2026 pattern is memory-based online adaptation: store each decision and its aftermath as a case, and at inference time retrieve the most relevant past cases so the model conditions on them. Formalized as a memory-augmented decision process, this gives "continual, real-time adaptation without gradient updates," and adds several points of accuracy on novel tasks purely through better recall. Your corrections become retrievable lessons, not weight updates — so the next night's read reflects what you taught it, and nothing silently rots the base model.

★ Governance — so the loop improves the engine instead of corrupting it

Evolving memory has a known failure mode: errors compound when a feedback loop feeds on itself. Four guardrails keep it honest. (1) Outcome-gated: a correction only hardens into a rule once outcomes confirm it — opinions don't, results do. (2) Bounded & decaying: validity windows + a half-life mean stale regimes fade; the rulebook is capped at a dozen lines. (3) Memory can't override safety: no retrieved lesson can open the 200-day trend gate or conjure a number with no source. (4) Audited: the dashboard's memory_used block shows exactly which cases moved the lean, so you can prune a bad lesson the moment you see it. Memory is a humble adviser the model consults — never an autopilot.

07 / THE EMERGING-THEME RADAR

Finding satellites before the crowdSpotting a theme while it's still a weak signal — now cross-checked against the graph, not just the text stream.

The frame is weak-signal analysis (Ansoff): every big trend begins as faint, ambiguous noise. The job is to sort the text stream into noise → weak → strong and promote only weak signals with a real catalyst underneath. The canonical detector scores a topic by how much is written and how fast that's growing — flagging small-but-accelerating topics for review.

★ The promotion rule

Promote Emerging → Watchlist only on low base + high acceleration + a corroborated catalyst + an investable wrapper + not-yet-crowded. That precise combination separates a durable emerging theme from a hype flash. The graph cross-check (step 4) is the new guard against a single loud source masquerading as breadth.

Lifecycle example: AI-compute optical modules were a textbook weak signal in early 2024 (small base, accelerating 研报 volume, a real hyperscaler-capex catalyst) — exactly when the radar would flag them. By mid-2026 it's the opposite: maximum crowding, "new stock king" covers; the crowding penalty now dominates and the same engine says do-not-chase. That round-trip is the system working.

08 / THE DECISION LAYER

States → a calibrated, conditional leanThe states are legible; the LLM turns them into a defensible verdict — without the confident-nonsense failure mode, and now humbled by your own past calls.

The literature is blunt: language models are systematically overconfident, worse in finance and worse in non-English — they favour plausible text over honest uncertainty. The states-not-composite design is the first defence (a legible band can't people-please); the decision layer adds the rest, and §06's retrieved cases add a memory of when this exact setup fooled you before.

The macro bridge & lead relay (recap)

Above each theme sits the liquidity layer that moves all risk assets (state 8: Fed path, 10-yr real yield, dollar, USD/CNY) — it rarely triggers a trade; it sets the book-wide risk_dial. Below it runs the universal lead chain, each arrow a lead you can act on before the local names price it: end-demand capex → bellwether vendor guidance → tier-1 supplier guide vs whisper → US-listed proxies → A-share names → local earnings (the laggard). In the graph these are LEADS edges; as a factor it's state 1 (cross-market lead).

The calibration discipline

  • Consider-the-opposite — force a bull node and a bear node, then synthesize. Deliberation beats a polite single prompt for calibration.
  • Base-rate first — state the relevant base rate (champion's curse; what parabolic themes historically give back) before the conviction.
  • Allocation > timing; sin only a little — default to Hold/low conviction; lean hard only at genuine extremes. Factor timing is harder than market timing.
  • Evidence-grounded & auto-downgrade — every field cites a fetched, dated source; conflict/staleness/single-source/forum-only lowers confidence and is logged. No source → omit; never invent a number.
  • Abstain when blind — too many invalid states, or extremes pointing opposite with no resolution → Abstain, not a guess.

GATE: if trend_gate is below 200-day MA, cap the lean at Hold/Avoid no matter the states — and no retrieved lesson may open this gate. I read this in the morning, leave a one-tap reaction, and size from my capped sleeve.
09 / STORAGE

A local-first store that remembers what it knew, whenThe v2 plan said "DuckDB + Parquet." That still works — but a 2026 retail engine can do better, and the upgrade is what makes both point-in-time honesty and the connection graph cheap.

Three different shapes of data flow through this engine, and one mistake — storing them all the same way — is what makes look-ahead bias and stale signals creep in. Prices and factors are time-series that get revised; holdings and drivers are a graph that changes each quarter; the LLM's reasoning and your feedback are text + vectors. Each gets the embedded, no-server engine built for it. Nothing here needs a database administrator or a cloud bill — it all runs from a folder on your machine.

⛁ The one rule that ties the store together

Point-in-time, everywhere. Prices, factor states, and the holdings graph all carry an as-of stamp. When the engine reasons tonight, it sees only what was knowable tonight; when you replay last month, it sees only last month. Bitemporal time-series (ArcticDB) + snapshotted graph + time-travel SQL (DuckLake) means every layer can answer "what did we know, and when did we know it" — which is the whole game in not lying to yourself about a signal.

You do not need all four on day one. The honest minimum to start is DuckDB + Parquet (exactly the v2 plan) plus a single LanceDB folder for the LLM's memory. Add ArcticDB the moment you start backtesting factor states across revisions; add the graph DB when list 4 — connections — goes live. The point is that each upgrade is additive and local, never a migration to a server you have to babysit.

10 / DURABILITY

Keeping it honest for yearsThree things rot a system like this: an overfit factor set, a memory that drifts, and signals that quietly stop working. Each has a specific defense.

The v3 design starts with a structural advantage: by emitting descriptive factor states the LLM reads rather than a fitted composite score (§03), there are far fewer knobs to overfit. You are not tuning eight weights until a backtest gleams — you are reporting whether each factor is high, neutral, or low against logic-based bands. Overfitting is a disease of optimization; the cure is having little to optimize. But the moment you backtest anything — a band, a gate, the rotation itself — the quant literature's defenses apply, so bake them in.

DisciplineWhat it protects against
Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR)Corrects a result's Sharpe for how many variants you tried plus non-normality. A gleaming in-sample Sharpe is a statistical mirage if it's the best of 100 trials — DSR tells you whether it survives that correction.
Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO)Via combinatorially-purged CV: estimates the chance your chosen config ranks below median out-of-sample. High PBO means you fit the noise, not the signal.
Purged / embargoed CV (CPCV)Cross-validation that purges overlapping labels and embargoes a time gap so future data can't leak backward into training. Preferred over plain walk-forward whenever labels span multiple days.
Parameter-stability scoreThe share of nearby settings (±10–20% on any band) that stay profitable. Above ~70–80% means a robust plateau, not a knife-edge fit you'll fall off live.
After-cost & point-in-time, alwaysReport after fees/slippage; reason only on as-of data (§09). The bitemporal store exists precisely to kill leakage-driven inflation — use it, don't bypass it for convenience.
⊘ Governing the memory so it learns without drifting

An evolving memory can accumulate errors — a wrong lesson, once stored, can be retrieved forever and quietly compound. The §06 loop is built against exactly this. Four rules, non-negotiable: (1) outcome-gated promotion — a case becomes a durable lesson only after its outcome is labelled, never on your reaction alone; (2) validity windows + decay — lessons carry a freshness date and fade unless re-confirmed, so an old regime can't rule a new one; (3) memory never overrides the safety gate or invents a number — no retrieved lesson can open the 200-day trend gate or fabricate data; (4) every lesson is audited — it shows up in the dashboard's memory_used block, so you can always see, and veto, what the system "learned."

★ The durability rules of thumb

Prefer states to a tuned blend. Prefer robust, well-documented factors — reversal, turnover/illiquidity, the cross-market lead — to exotic ones you found yesterday. Treat any auto-discovered factor or GNN link-prediction as a candidate for human review and out-of-sample testing, never a drop-in. And remember factors decay as they crowd: momentum's premium has thinned for years; crowding now signals crash risk more than alpha. So re-check the engine's own hit-rate quarterly, watch the dispersion, and retire what stops working before it costs you.

13 / LIMITS & DISCIPLINE

What it is, and isn'tRe-read whenever the dashboard sounds certain. The new pieces — connections, a self-correcting memory — add power and new ways to fool yourself.

▲ What the engine gives you

  • A durable process: the same disciplined nightly read on any theme, for years — states and evidence, not a vibe.
  • Connection awareness: when a holding falls, an exact query shows every fund on your lists exposed to it — contagion you'd otherwise miss.
  • Less lag: the US→China relay and leading factors buy days-to-weeks over a crowd watching reported earnings.
  • A system that learns you: a governed memory that stops repeating the mistakes you flagged yesterday.

▼ What it cannot do

  • Predict tops/bottoms. Leads and graphs cut lag and surface links — they don't remove uncertainty.
  • Trust a recalled number. Fetch-don't-recall; the memory stores lessons, never figures to quote back.
  • Outlast its own discipline. Factors decay; a memory can drift; an overfit band rots. Govern and re-validate.
  • Replace your judgment. A tool that agrees faster — or now, one that "remembers" agreeing — is more dangerous than none.
The shape of using it well

Funds/ETFs only, satellites capped, the boring broad-index core compounding underneath. The connection graph and the feedback loop don't change the goal — they sharpen it. The engine's gift was never more trades; it's better-timed, fewer, calmer ones, each with a written reason, each checked against what you've learned. Most mornings the right action is none. Patience is a position.

Self-correcting memory & the feedback loop

Learning from feedback without fine-tuning: memory-based online adaptation — store {state → lean → feedback → outcome} as cases, retrieve similar ones, condition the next decision; reported out-of-sample gains with no gradient updates (AgentFly / Memento line, arXiv 2508.16153).

Factors & the 2026 evidence that shaped the states

Crowding = risk, not alpha: crowded factors show materially higher crash probability while crowding-based selection earns little; treat crowding as a crash gauge, not a return source (arXiv 2512.11913).

Reversal's mechanism: turnover/liquidity and Amihud illiquidity are consistent drivers of short-term reversal — the structural reason reversal dominates in the A-share market (PMC5125680; Amihud 2002).

Momentum, decayed & crowded: the momentum premium has thinned over decades and sits at wide dispersion (e.g. JP Morgan Factor Views, 2Q 2026); short-term reversal is the robust exception (Ehsani & Linnainmaa, NBER). Simplicity holds up: equal-weight (1/N) beats many optimizers out-of-sample (DeMiguel, Garlappi & Uppal).
