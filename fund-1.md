# Building Wealth on a Salary: An Evidence-Based Guide to Funds and Alternatives in the U.S. and China

*A research report for a time-constrained investor targeting top-decile outcomes. Prepared June 2026.*

> **Important:** This is educational research, not personalized financial or tax advice. I am not a licensed financial advisor. The figures below are drawn from public data (sourced at the end) to help you make your own informed decisions. Markets change, and your residency, tax status, and risk tolerance materially change what's appropriate. Consider consulting a fee-only fiduciary advisor before acting.

---

## 1. Read this first: what "top-decile outcomes" can and cannot mean

Your goal has three words doing a lot of work — *above-average*, *top-decile*, and *lower-risk* — combined with *minimal time*. Before any market-specific advice, it's worth separating three goals that sound similar but are very different, because the evidence treats them very differently.

**Goal A — Above-average returns relative to other investors.** This is highly achievable, and the path is well-documented. Low-cost, broadly diversified index funds reliably beat *most* other investors, including most professionals, over long periods. That is genuinely "above average."

**Goal B — Top-decile *annual returns* (beating ~90% of portfolios each year).** This is essentially incompatible with "lower-risk" and "minimal time." Top-decile returns require concentration, leverage, or luck — and the same forces that could put you in the top 10% can put you in the bottom 10%. There is no evidence-backed, low-risk, low-effort recipe for consistently top-decile *returns*. Anyone selling one is selling a story.

**Goal C — Top-decile *wealth* (net worth) over a career.** This is realistic for a disciplined salaried person — but the dominant levers are your **savings rate** and **time invested**, not your return-picking skill. For context, U.S. thresholds in recent data: roughly **$251,000** in annual household income is the top 10% by income, and roughly **$1.8–1.9 million** in net worth is the top 10% by wealth (Federal Reserve SCF / DQYDJ / Visa-CNBC analysis). The top 1% by net worth is about **$13.7 million**.

The single most useful reframe in this whole report: **for someone like you, top-decile wealth is mostly a savings-and-time problem with market returns as the engine — not a trading problem.** And here the evidence is encouraging: the biggest destroyer of investor returns isn't picking the wrong fund, it's *behavior* — buying high, panic-selling low, and trying to time the market. Studies repeatedly find the average investor earns several percentage points *less* than the funds they own. A "set-it-and-forget-it" approach isn't a compromise forced by your schedule; **it is the strategy with the best evidence behind it.** Your lack of time is an advantage.

So the rest of this report answers your questions honestly: funds (specifically low-cost index funds) are the best *vehicle* for the engine. The "comparable durable, high-ROI, lower-risk" alternatives exist but sit on a trade-off curve — you cannot maximize all three at once.

## 2. The United States

### 2.1 Is fund investing the best way? Yes — but the *type* of fund is everything

The most robust finding in all of personal finance is that **most actively managed funds lose to a simple index over time, and the failure rate rises the longer you wait.** S&P's SPIVA scorecards have measured this for over 20 years:

- In 2025, about **79% of active U.S. large-cap funds underperformed the S&P 500**.
- Over a **20-year** horizon, roughly **92% of U.S. domestic equity funds** underperformed their benchmarks.
- Over the **15-year** period ending 2024, there was **not a single one of 22 U.S. equity categories** in which a majority of active managers beat their benchmark.

Two honest caveats. First, active management does occasionally win in pockets — small-caps had an unusually strong year in 2024, and *active bond funds* (especially short/intermediate investment-grade) have done better than the equity story suggests. Second, an industry-funded academic critique (sponsored by the Investment Adviser Association) argues SPIVA overstates the case by weighting all funds equally and treating every closed fund as a failure; on an asset-weighted basis, active fixed income looks meaningfully better. But the core conclusion for *equities* is not seriously disputed: **for stock exposure, low-cost index funds are the high-probability choice.**

Why index funds win comes down to arithmetic, not magic: as a group, investors earn the market return *minus costs*. Active funds charge more (often 0.5%–1.0%+ per year) and trade more (taxes, spreads). A broad index fund charges almost nothing. That cost gap compounds relentlessly.

**What this means concretely for you:** the "fund" you want is a *broad, low-cost index fund or ETF*, not a star-manager active fund. Total-U.S.-market and S&P 500 index funds from the major low-cost providers carry expense ratios around **0.015%–0.03%** — i.e., $1.50–$3.00 per year per $10,000. A typical active fund can cost 20–60× that.

### 2.2 What returns are reasonable to expect

Over nearly a century (since 1926), U.S. large-cap stocks have returned roughly **10% per year nominally** and about **7% per year after inflation**, with dividends reinvested (Shiller data; multiple compilers). Crucially, **no 20-year holding period in that history has lost money**, even through the Depression, stagflation, 2008, and COVID. But the ride is violent: single years swing from +30% to −38%, and there have been "lost decades" (the 2000s returned roughly *nothing* before dividends). The long-run average is the reward for tolerating that volatility.

One warning for the decade ahead: U.S. valuations are historically high, and several forecasters expect lower-than-historical returns going forward. Treat 7% real as a long-run anchor, not a promise for any given decade.

### 2.3 The "comparable, durable, high-ROI, lower-risk" alternatives — and the trade-off

Here is the honest core: **durable + high-ROI + low-risk is a trilemma.** You can have any two. The table below shows long-run U.S. asset-class returns (largely from Aswath Damodaran's NYU dataset, 1928–2024) so you can see the trade-off directly.

| Asset class | Long-run nominal return | Volatility / risk | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| U.S. stocks (S&P 500) | ~10% | High (−38% in a bad year) | Growth engine |
| Equity REITs (real estate securities) | ~11–12% total return (1972–2019) | High, stock-like | Growth + income + diversification |
| Corporate bonds (investment-grade) | ~5–7% | Moderate | Income, ballast |
| 10-yr Treasury bonds | ~4.5–5% | Low–moderate | Safety, deflation hedge |
| Gold | ~5% | High, "feast or famine" | Crisis/inflation hedge |
| Direct real estate (price only) | ~4.3% | Low price volatility but illiquid | Leverage + tangible asset |
| Cash / T-bills | ~2–3% | Very low | Liquidity, dry powder |
| (Inflation, for reference) | ~3% | — | The hurdle to beat |

A few takeaways:

- **REITs are the standout "comparable to stocks" option.** Publicly traded real estate has historically matched or beaten the S&P 500 on *total* return while adding diversification and income — and unlike a rental property, a REIT index fund requires zero management and is fully liquid. (Note: the raw "real estate ~4.3%" figure excludes rental income and leverage, which is why direct property can still work well for hands-on owners — but that fails your "minimal time" test.)
- **"Lower-risk" almost always means "lower-ROI."** Treasuries, TIPS, I-bonds, money-market funds, and high-grade bond funds are the durable, low-risk options — and right now they're unusually attractive. As of June 2026 the Fed funds rate is **3.50%–3.75%**, the 3-month T-bill yields about **3.75%**, the 10-year Treasury about **4.46%**, and large money-market funds yield in the high-3% range. That's a real, low-risk ~4% with essentially no effort. It won't build top-decile wealth on its own, but it's a legitimate "durable + low-risk" anchor and a far better deal than the equivalent in China (see §3).
- **The best risk-adjusted, low-effort answer is a *blend*, not a single asset.** A globally diversified stock index fund as the engine, a bond/Treasury allocation sized to your time horizon as the ballast, and optionally a REIT and small inflation-hedge sleeve. This is the entire premise of low-cost target-date and "three-fund" portfolios.

### 2.4 The biggest U.S.-specific lever: tax-advantaged accounts

For a salaried worker, *where* you hold funds can matter as much as which funds. Employer 401(k)s (especially with matching — an immediate, risk-free return), IRAs/Roth IRAs, and HSAs shelter compounding from taxes for decades. Maxing tax-advantaged space with low-cost index funds is, for most U.S. employees, the highest-expected-value, lowest-effort move available. This is a structural advantage the China side largely lacks.

## 3. China

The China answer is genuinely different — and more cautionary. Two facts dominate everything.

### 3.1 The two pillars of Chinese household wealth have both disappointed

**Pillar 1 — Equities: "growth without returns."** Despite China's economy growing faster than almost any major country for three decades, its stock market has been one of the *worst*-performing in the world over the long run (academic studies of the Shanghai Composite, 1992–2014). The most striking illustration: the **CSI 300** (China's blue-chip index) hit an all-time high near **5,877 in October 2007** and, as of June 2026, sits around **4,748 — still below its 2007 peak nearly 19 years later.** Over the same span the S&P 500 multiplied several times over. Chinese equities are also prone to violent bubble-and-crash cycles (a 573% run then ~70% crash around 2007; a ~150% run then ~40%+ crash in 2015), partly because the market is **retail-dominated and widely considered less efficient** than developed markets, with 10% daily price limits and frequent trading halts. (There has been a sharp rebound recently — the CSI 300 is up roughly 23% year-on-year on stimulus — but the long-run record is the relevant context.)

**Pillar 2 — Property: a multi-year crisis.** This matters enormously because roughly **70% of urban Chinese household wealth is held in residential property** — housing assets are about **5× GDP**, far more concentrated than in the U.S. or Japan (Brookings; GAM; NBS). Since the Evergrande collapse around 2020–2021, the property market has been in its **fifth-to-sixth year of decline** with no clear bottom: prices were still falling year-on-year through 2025–2026 (first-tier cities down ~4–5%, lower-tier cities worse), and ratings agencies project further declines in 2026. The result is a negative wealth effect that has pushed households toward cash — bank deposits have roughly doubled in five years.

So the two traditional stores of Chinese household wealth have *both* let savers down for years. That is the backdrop for any China allocation.

### 3.2 Is fund investing the best way in China? More complicated

There's a popular belief that because China's market is less efficient and retail-driven, active managers can more easily outperform. **This used to be true and is rapidly ceasing to be true.**

- Three years ago, *over 60%* of active China equity funds beat their passive peers.
- By **2024, only about 13%** did — a collapse of ~52 percentage points in a single year (Morningstar China Active/Passive Barometer).
- UBS reports **67% of active managers underperformed the CSI 300 over the past three years**, and at the end of 2024, **Chinese ETFs' A-share holdings overtook those of active equity mutual funds for the first time.** The ETF industry has grown ~40% per year since 2005, and regulators are actively pushing index investing (even tying fund-manager pay to multi-year performance).

A unique wrinkle: China's **"national team"** — state-backed funds that buy ETFs to stabilize the market — is now a major force, which both supports passive flows and adds a layer of policy-driven price action you don't get in the U.S.

**Bottom line for China funds:** the active-vs-passive verdict is *converging toward the U.S. pattern* (passive winning), but the results are noisier year-to-year, and the whole equity market carries structural risks the U.S. doesn't.

### 3.3 The "comparable, durable, lower-risk" alternatives in China — and why they're weaker

This is where the contrast with the U.S. is sharpest.

| | United States | China |
|---|---|---|
| "Risk-free" short rate | Fed funds ~3.5–3.75%; T-bills ~3.75% | 1-yr bank deposit **~0.95%** |
| 10-year government bond | ~4.46% | **~1.7–1.9%** |
| "Safe" yield available with little effort | ~4% (money markets/Treasuries) | ~1–2% |

- **Cash and government bonds yield almost nothing.** With one-year deposits at major state banks around **0.95%** and 10-year government bonds near **1.7–1.9%**, China is in a low-rate, arguably "Japanification"-risk environment. Safe assets barely beat (or trail) the cost of living.
- **Bank Wealth Management Products (理财, WMPs) are no longer the guaranteed product they once were.** This is a critical, under-appreciated change. The WMP market is enormous (over **¥31 trillion / ~$4.3 trillion**) and growing as deposits look unattractive, but since the 2018 "asset management new rules" (资管新规), the old **implicit guarantee was deliberately removed**: WMPs are now mark-to-market (NAV-based) and explicitly *not* principal-protected. The same shift is happening in the bond market, where the government has begun letting even state-linked issuers default (breaking the long-standing assumption of a state backstop). The "safe, high-yield" Chinese product many savers remember **no longer exists in that form.**
- **Gold** has become a popular store of value for Chinese households (and the central bank), reflecting exactly this search for something durable amid weak property, weak equities, and low rates.

### 3.4 Access and capital controls (this changes what's even possible)

This is decisive and depends entirely on your residency:

- **If you are a mainland China resident:** you face a **$50,000 per person, per year** foreign-exchange "facilitation quota," and — importantly — **buying overseas securities is not officially a permitted use of it.** Rules tightened further on January 1, 2026, specifically cracking down on splitting transfers across family members. The legitimate channels for global exposure are **QDII funds** (domestic funds licensed to invest abroad, the main legal route), Hong Kong–routed accounts, and Stock/Bond Connect. So a China-based investor's access to U.S. index funds is *constrained and indirect.*
- **If you are a U.S. (or other foreign) resident:** you reach China most simply through **U.S.-listed China ETFs** (broad China, large-cap, or China-tech variants), **ADRs**, or, for institutions, Stock Connect / the QFII scheme. You generally face dividend withholding and currency risk, but capital gains on publicly traded securities are typically not U.S.-taxed for non-residents in the reverse direction.

### 3.5 The risk overlay you can't diversify away

Beyond returns, China carries **policy and governance risks** that are hard to model: sudden regulatory crackdowns (e.g., the 2021 actions against tech and private education that erased enormous value overnight), state intervention in prices, currency controls limiting your ability to get money *out*, and lower corporate-disclosure transparency. None of these are reasons to avoid China entirely — but they are strong reasons to treat it as a *measured satellite holding*, not a core, and to demand a higher expected return for bearing them.

## 4. Are investment approaches in China similar to those in the U.S.?

Partly, and increasingly so on one dimension — but they differ profoundly on others.

| Dimension | United States | China |
|---|---|---|
| Active vs. passive verdict | Passive wins decisively | **Now converging to "passive wins"** (active's old edge has collapsed) |
| Market efficiency | Relatively high | Lower; more predictable patterns, more retail noise |
| Dominant investor | Institutions | Historically retail; institutionalizing |
| State's role in markets | Minimal/indirect | Heavy: "national team," interventions, policy shocks |
| Risk-free rate | ~4% | ~1–2% |
| Currency & capital mobility | USD freely convertible | RMB controlled; $50k/yr personal quota |
| Disclosure/transparency | High | Lower |
| Long-run equity record | Strong (~7% real) | Weak ("growth without returns") |
| Tax-advantaged retirement accounts | Extensive (401k/IRA/HSA) | Limited |

**The convergence:** in *both* markets, the evidence now points the same way — low-cost, broad, passive index exposure beats most active alternatives, and the low-effort approach is the high-probability approach. A Chinese investor and an American investor would both be well-served by the same *philosophy* (low cost, broad diversification, long horizon, don't trade).

**The divergence that matters for strategy:** the *raw material* is different. U.S. equities have a multi-decade record of rewarding patient holders; Chinese equities have a multi-decade record of *not* doing so despite stellar GDP growth. The risk-free fallback is ~4% in the U.S. versus ~1% in China. And capital controls plus policy risk mean a China-based investor and a foreign investor face entirely different opportunity sets.

**The practical implication:** for almost anyone whose goal is durable, lower-risk, top-decile *wealth* with minimal effort, **China is best held as a diversifier inside a globally diversified portfolio, not as a concentrated bet.** Global diversification (rather than betting on any single country, including the U.S.) is itself one of the few genuinely "free" risk reductions available.

## 5. Putting it together: a low-effort framework

This is illustrative structure, not a recommendation — calibrate to your own situation and (ideally) a fiduciary's input.

**The engine (most of the work is here, and it isn't trading):**
1. **Save aggressively and automatically.** Your savings rate and years invested will determine your decile far more than fund selection. Automate contributions so the decision is made once.
2. **Fill tax-advantaged space first** (if U.S.: 401(k) to at least the match, then IRA/Roth/HSA), in low-cost index funds.
3. **Use a broad, low-cost equity index fund as the core** — ideally a *total-world* or U.S.+international combination, so you own global growth (including a measured China slice) without making country bets.

**The ballast (sized to your time horizon):**
4. **Add high-quality bonds / Treasuries** as you get closer to needing the money or to reduce volatility. Today's ~4% U.S. yields make this ballast more rewarding than it's been in years.
5. **Optional diversifiers:** a REIT index sleeve (real-estate exposure without being a landlord) and a small inflation hedge (TIPS/I-bonds, perhaps a little gold).

**The discipline (this is the part that actually fails for most people):**
6. **Rebalance once or twice a year**, and otherwise *do nothing.* Don't react to headlines. The behavior gap — not fund choice — is what separates real investor returns from index returns.

**Effort budget:** Set up correctly, this is a few hours initially and perhaps an hour or two per year thereafter — which fits your "don't waste time daily" constraint and, per the evidence, is *better* than doing more.

## 6. Key caveats and limits

- **Not advice.** I'm not a financial advisor; this is research to inform your own decisions. For anything tax- or residency-specific, consult a fee-only fiduciary and/or a cross-border tax professional.
- **Residency drives everything.** A U.S. person and a mainland-China resident have very different access, tax treatment, and currency exposure. Confirm what you can legally and practically buy before planning around it.
- **Past performance ≠ future results.** U.S. valuations are high; the next decade's returns may be lower than the historical 7% real. China's long-run record is weak but its assets are cheaper. Neither past pattern is guaranteed to continue.
- **Currency risk is real in both directions.** Holding U.S. assets exposes a China-based investor to RMB/USD swings (and vice versa). The ability to *repatriate* funds also differs sharply between the two systems.
- **"Top-decile returns" remains the wrong target.** Aim for top-decile *wealth* via savings + time + low-cost global diversification + behavioral discipline. That is the version of your goal the evidence actually supports.
